# TowerGuard — Vision

## The Problem

Perimeter security has a human endurance problem.

A soldier, guard, or contractor assigned to a fixed observation post gets tired. Attention drifts. Coverage gaps emerge. Response timelines stretch. In high-stakes environments — forward operating bases, critical infrastructure, border installations — those gaps create risk.

Today's drone-based solutions don't fix this. A single drone running patrols requires a pilot, has a 20–30 minute battery window, and creates its own downtime gap during recharge. You've replaced one tired human with one battery-limited machine.

**TowerGuard is a different architecture.**

---

## The Solution

An autonomous nest deployed at the perimeter site.

Inside: 3–4 low-cost drones in permanent rotation.

- One drone is always airborne
- One is always ready on the pad
- One is charging
- One is in maintenance reserve

The nest handles launch, recovery, charging, and fleet coordination autonomously. The operator monitors a dashboard. The AI flags events. The human decides whether to act.

**Coverage becomes persistent, not episodic.**

## Doctrine

**Persistent over episodic.** A 20-minute drone flight is an event. TowerGuard is a condition — the perimeter is always watched.

**Autonomy in the loop, not in command.** The AI detects and flags. The human authorizes. Manual takeover is always one click away.

**Platform before hardware.** The software orchestration layer is the durable asset. Hardware is a commodity. The platform integrates with whatever drone fleet the customer has.

**Sensor-agnostic from day one.** Drones are the initial sensor layer. Thermal cameras, fixed cameras, radar, RF detection, acoustic sensors — all feed the same platform. TowerGuard grows with the sensor budget.
